PRD: Mid-Ranked Improvement Tooling

Samuel Bingham · Product Requirements Doc · July 2026

A full spec for Opportunity 1 from my TFT product teardown -- a post-game analysis layer for Platinum through Diamond players. The teardown made the case for why. This is the doc that would actually get it built.

Overview

TargetSet 18 launch window--
Related workTeardown, Opportunity 1 · SQL self-analysis (proof of concept, see Appendix)--

The teardown argued that Platinum through Diamond players churn at a rate their engagement level doesn't predict, and that the root cause is a feedback gap: the post-game screen shows placement and damage, and nothing else. Players in this bracket know they're losing. They don't have a way to find out why, so improvement in this range is a slow, unguided grind, and a meaningful number of engaged players quit instead of pushing through it.

This doc turns that argument into something buildable: a post-game analysis layer that surfaces specific, personalized decision patterns correlated with worse outcomes, instead of generic tips. The hard part was never the data -- Riot already has everything needed to compute this. The hard part is presentation: making it read like a coach's insight instead of a report card, at the exact moment a player is still receptive to it. Most of this spec is about that presentation problem, not the data pipeline.

Problem Statement

Mid-ranked players (roughly Platinum through Diamond, based on the teardown's segmentation) have no structured mechanism for identifying what's holding their climb back. The skills that separate this bracket from the one above it -- rolldown timing, pivot recognition, late-game positioning -- are exactly the skills that are hardest to self-diagnose from a placement number alone. A player who goes 7th can tell they lost. They can't tell whether it was itemization, econ, or a positioning mistake in the final fight, and the game gives them no way to find out short of reviewing their own memory of the match.

The result is a bracket that churns not because players dislike the game, but because their rate of improvement doesn't match their invested effort, and unguided effort with no visible payoff is exactly the condition that produces quiet attrition rather than loud complaints. That makes it dangerous from a retention standpoint: there's no support ticket to read, no forum thread to notice. The players who'd benefit most from this feature are the ones least likely to tell anyone why they left.

Goals & Non-Goals

Goals

  • Give Platinum-Diamond players at least one specific, personalized, actionable insight per session, drawn from their own recent match history.
  • Make the insight feel like coaching, not grading -- comparative and constructive framing, never a raw list of mistakes.
  • Keep the feature fully opt-in, so it never becomes an unwanted evaluation layer for players who don't want one.
  • Ship an MVP that reuses data Riot's systems already compute, so this is a presentation and surfacing problem first, not a new-data-pipeline problem.

Non-Goals

  • Not a full replay/VOD review system -- that's the separate, heavier Opportunity 6 in the teardown, and this doc treats it as future work, not a dependency.
  • Not targeted at Challenger/Grandmaster players, who already have external tools (MetaTFT, tactics.tools) and a community built around self-diagnosis. This is specifically for the bracket that doesn't have that support structure yet.
  • Not a coaching marketplace or matchmaking-by-skill-gap feature -- purely a self-serve insight layer.

Target Users

The "invested improver": a Platinum-Diamond player who plays multiple games a week, tracks their own rank, and wants to climb -- but has hit a plateau they can't explain.

This player already cares enough to be a retention risk worth solving for -- they're not a lapsed casual, they're actively trying and stalling. They're distinct from two adjacent segments this feature explicitly isn't for: the casual player who plays for fun and would find any evaluation unwelcome (addressed directly in the Caveat section of the teardown), and the high-elo player who already has external tooling and community context to self-diagnose. The mid-ranked improver is the one segment currently getting nothing.

User Stories

  • As a Platinum player who keeps finishing 5th-8th, I want to know if there's a pattern to when I'm losing (early rolldowns, late-game positioning, itemization) so I'm not guessing at what to practice.
  • As a player who just had a bad game, I want the option to see what separated my board from a top-4 finish, framed as "here's what worked for other players in your lobby" rather than a list of my mistakes.
  • As a player who doesn't want to be evaluated every time I open the client, I want this feature to stay out of my way until I actively ask for it.
  • As a returning player after a break, I want to see whether my recent dip in placement traces to a specific habit, rather than assuming I've just "lost my touch."

Scope & Phasing

The full version of this idea (arbitrary natural-language coaching, augment-aware recommendations, trend tracking over a full season) is a multi-quarter investment. The MVP scope below is deliberately narrow: it should be buildable from data Riot already computes, and it should be enough to test the core hypothesis -- that personalized signal changes retention -- before committing further.

Design Principles

The strongest counterargument to this whole feature is that TFT's casual audience does not want to feel evaluated when they open the game. Every principle below is a direct response to that risk, not an afterthought.

Opt-in, always. The insight layer never appears automatically. It's a single, low-friction prompt on the post-game screen ("See what separated this game from a top-4 finish?"), dismissible with one tap, and it doesn't reappear pushily if dismissed repeatedly.

Comparative, not absolute. Every insight is framed relative to the lobby or to the player's own recent history -- "players who beat you rolled down two rounds earlier on average" -- never "you made a mistake." This is a direct requirement pulled from the teardown's caveat section, not a nice-to-have.

Timed for receptiveness, not just accuracy. The insight has to land while the player is still turning the game over in their head, not three menus deep into queuing the next one. If the prompt requires more than one click to reach, it's already too late.

Explicit non-target: this system should never be tuned to maximize engagement with itself. If a player dismisses it every game, that's a valid, permanent signal to stop showing it, not a retention problem to solve with better hooks.

Success Metrics

Primary

  • Week-over-week retention, Platinum-Diamond bracket, opted-in vs. opted-out cohorts
  • Average placement change over a player's next 10 games after receiving an insight, vs. a matched control who didn't

Secondary

  • Average session count per player, Platinum-Diamond bracket
  • Opt-in rate, and repeat opt-in rate after the first exposure
  • Self-reported satisfaction in a lightweight post-game survey ("was this helpful?")

Guardrails

  • Dismissal rate must not climb over time within a cohort -- rising dismissal is an early signal the framing has drifted toward "report card"
  • No measurable retention drop in the casual/non-opted-in population -- the feature must be provably invisible to players who don't want it

Rollout Plan

Phase 0 -- Internal validation. Before shipping any UI, validate that the rolldown-timing and comparative-econ signals actually correlate with placement at a strength worth surfacing. This is exactly the kind of check my SQL analysis of my own games ran informally and by hand -- see the Appendix below for what that validation looks like in practice, and where it would have needed a bigger, real dataset instead of one account's history.

Phase 1 -- Opt-in beta, single region. Ship the MVP scope to a small opted-in cohort in one region. Primary goal: confirm the comparative-framing UI doesn't read as judgmental in practice, not just in spec. This is a qualitative check as much as a quantitative one -- pull a handful of beta users for direct feedback on how the framing actually feels.

Phase 2 -- Full Platinum-Diamond rollout. Expand to the full bracket once the beta clears both the primary metrics and the guardrails. Continue as opt-in; do not flip the default without a separate, explicit decision and its own review.

Phase 3 -- Evaluate Phase 2 scope (positioning signal, multi-game trends) based on what the MVP's single-signal data actually shows demand for, rather than building it speculatively now.

Risks & Open Questions

  • Framing risk: even well-intentioned comparative framing can read as judgmental to a player who just lost. This needs real user research before Phase 1, not just internal review -- the teardown flags this as an open question, and it still is one here.
  • Signal quality risk: a rolldown-timing or positioning signal that's only weakly correlated with placement would ship a feature that erodes trust the first time it gives bad advice. Phase 0 validation exists specifically to catch this before it reaches players.
  • Scope creep risk: the temptation to fold in trend tracking or positioning analysis into the MVP should be resisted -- each of those needs its own validation pass, and bundling them risks shipping something none of which is fully validated.
  • Open question: should this eventually connect to the Creator Dashboard idea (Opportunity 3) so high-elo creators can reference "what mid-ranked players actually struggle with" in their content? Interesting adjacency, deliberately out of scope for this doc.

Appendix: A Worked Example

One finding from my SQL analysis of my own Set 17 match history is a small, concrete preview of what this feature would need to do well. My Miss Fortune games (trait: Gun Goddess) averaged a 5.56 placement against a 4.38 baseline across the rest of my traits -- a sharp, real underperformance across sixteen games. But the data alone couldn't tell me why: itemization, positioning, and econ mistakes all look the same from placement and trait data alone. That gap -- a real, personalized signal with no way to explain its cause -- is precisely the problem this PRD's MVP is scoped to close, and precisely why the doc treats the comparative-econ and rolldown-timing signals as the first things worth building, not positioning or replay analysis: they're the signals actually computable today, without a much larger investment in tracking board state over time.

The honest caveat, same as in the SQL project itself: this is one account's worth of data. A real version of this feature would need Riot-scale data to validate that these signals hold up across thousands of players, not just one.

This PRD is a portfolio exercise, not an actual Riot document -- but every requirement in it traces back to a specific claim in the original teardown, and the appendix above is a real example pulled from my own SQL analysis. That's the point: the three pieces are meant to be read as one continuous chain -- diagnose the product problem, spec the fix, and show the underlying data literacy that the fix depends on.
